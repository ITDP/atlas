ITDP is committed to open-source data and algorithms. All of the code for the Atlas, backend and frontend , is available on GitHub under the MIT open source license (given in the License and Citation tab, above). All of the data that powers the calculations is available for at least free viewing and most is also available under an open license. The data in the Atlas is made available under the Open Database License: http://opendatacommons.org/licenses/odbl/1.0/ . Any rights in individual contents of the database are licensed under the Database Contents License: http://opendatacommons.org/licenses/dbcl/1.0/ . Data can be downloaded from individual city/indicator views. For larger data extracts, contact data@itdp.org .

The Atlas relies on four key sources of data:

  1. We use the European Commission’s Global Human Settlement Layer (GHSL) for the distribution of population and the boundaries of urban agglomerations.
    • Specifically, for population distribution (GHS-POP), we use the 2022 release. For urban agglomerations (the Urban Centre Database, GHS-UCDB), we use the 2019 release. We intend to upgrade as soon as a newer version is available.
    • The GHSL is available as free and open data under the Creative Commons CC BY 4.0 license. 
  2. We use OpenStreetMap (OSM) for the locations of streets, roads, pedestrian ways, bicycle ways, social services (healthcare and education), and the boundaries and names of legal jurisdictions.
    • OSM data is available under the Open Database License .
  3. We use Mobility Data’s Mobility Database for data on the operations of transit systems. This data is encoded in the General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) format, which is used by more than 10,000 transit operators worldwide.
    • The licenses of different GTFS files in the Mobility Database vary by operator.
    • GTFS is usually published by transit agencies, although in some cases it is collected by advocates, researchers, or private firms.
  4. We use the Transit Explorer database, maintained by Yonah Freemark and Steven Vance (with contributions from ITDP and other organizations and volunteers), for the locations and classifications of bus rapid transit, light rail, and metro lines and stations. 
    • Transit Explorer data can be viewed for free online or purchased for download.

A great advantage of OpenStreetMap, the Mobility Database, and Transit Explorer is that anyone—from a government official to a concerned citizen—can contribute to improving them and correct inaccuracies. 


Improving data

If you notice any data that is missing or incomplete, contact data@itdp.org or follow the steps below to add it yourself. Once you make changes to OpenStreetMap, the Mobility Database, or Transit Explorer, please contact us at data@itdp.org to inform us—the Atlas will not update automatically. We plan to update the data in the Atlas on an annual basis, but we may be able to update it more frequently for certain cities where change is rapid.


OpenStreetMap

Indicators: People Near Services, People Near Car-Free Places, Safety From Highways, People Near Bikeways

  1. Create or log into an existing OSM account. 
  2. Create, edit, or delete features as necessary to best reflect the actual conditions of the area. For detailed instructions on editing in OSM, use the walk-through available on the OSM help page or see the OpenStreetMap Wiki page.
    1. Examples of edits may include: locations of healthcare or education services, newly created pedestrian areas or paths, bikeways that are now physically protected
  3. Please note: Paths that cross each other (where it is possible to walk from one to the other) should have a shared node . OSM assumes it is not possible to walk between paths that cross each other without a shared node. This affects measurements requiring walking path distance.
  4. Bicycle tagging information:
    1. Ideally, physically protected bikeways are tagged as their own way (a unique feature), using highway=cycleway and cycleway=track rather than attributes of a way or feature representing a road or a street. 
      1. Physically protected bikeways that are labeled as cycleway=track (but not a separate way) will still be included in the People Near Bikeways indicator.
    2. Bikeways that are not physically protected (such as painted lanes on roads) should be labeled as highway=primary/secondary/etc and cycleway=lane. Unprotected bikeways are not included in the People Near Bikeways indicator but are included in a measurement of all bikeways. 

Further clarification on the distinction between lanes, tracks, and cycleways can be found here .


Mobility Database ( General Transit Feed Specification)

Indicators: People Near Frequent Transport

  1. If you already have GTFS data, you will have to add it to Mobility Data’s database .
  2. If you do not have GTFS data, you (or your city’s agency) will have to collect it, publish it, and add it to Mobility Data’s database.
    1. For more formal transport systems, which are documented and administered by a public agency, the data may already exist. You can use software like IBI Arcadis’s TRANSIT-data-tools to convert it to GTFS and publish it before adding it to Mobility Data’s database . TRANSIT-data-tools is free for use by cities in low- and middle-income countries.

For less formal transport systems, which are more often owner-operated or less regulated, you may have to lead an effort—or work with your local government—to collect the data in your city. You can develop your own approach, you can use open-source tools such as those provided by Trufi , or you can hire a specialized consultant like Trufi or TransportForCairo . If you hire a consultant, be sure to include in your contract that the data will be public and open, or you will not be able to add it to the Mobility Database or include it in the Atlas indicators .


Transit Explorer

Indicators: People Near Rapid Transport, People Near Frequent Transport

Transit Explorer provides information on fixed-guideway transit projects globally. Errors should be directly reported to yfreemark@thetransportpolitic.com or by using the form on the website and clicking “Find an error?”
