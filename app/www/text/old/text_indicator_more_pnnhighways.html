INDICATOR
Safety from Highways

Highways bring many forms of harm to people, and especially to people who live close to them:

  • Large numbers of polluting vehicles emit huge amounts of air pollution, and the areas within 500m of a highway suffer the most harm. This pollution can cause asthma, cardiovascular disease, and death.
  • Noise pollution is the second-most harmful type of environmental pollution for human health. Consistent loud noise, as is found near highways, can cause or worsen cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, mental health issues, and low birth weight.
  • Highways form barriers between neighborhoods, either preventing people from moving between them or forcing people to risk death on their daily errands. Road fatalities are the eighth-leading cause of death worldwide, and the only non-medical cause in the top ten.
  • Highways also lead car traffic to increase in nearby neighborhoods as people drive to or from access ramps. This causes air pollution, noise pollution, and traffic violence throughout nearby neighborhoods.

Although electric cars will lessen some of these problems, they still cause some air pollution and some noise - and they are no less dangerous on the road.

These problems are caused by all highways and arterial roads, but they are especially bad when they are caused by grade-separated limited-access highways.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
To identify grade-separated highways, we first identify all roads labeled in OpenStreetMap as primary, motorway, or trunk. Then we find all parts of those highways with at least 1500m between four-way intersections, meaning those parts where traffic can flow freely and is not stopped by signals or crossings. After identifying these grade-separated roads, we count the number of people living at least 500m from the nearest one.
Policy Recommendations
  • Immediately stop allocating money to highway construction. Once a highway is built, it is immediately harmful and difficult to remove.
  • Begin dismantling highways and replacing them with public transit, boulevards, and parks. This process has been successful in cities as diverse as Seoul, San Francisco, Paris, and Montreal. It has recently become a national policy priority in the United States.
  • Establish city-level or national-level transportation plans to not build, or to remove, urban highways.
