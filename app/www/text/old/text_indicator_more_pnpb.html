INDICATOR
People Near Protected Bikeways

The majority of people are interested in bicycling more, but choose not to bicycle because they do not have safe infrastructure to get where they want to go.

In cities that provide extensive, well-connected networks of physically-connected bicycle lanes, more people ride bicycles to get around. This benefits those people: it saves them time and money, while providing exercise to help them live more healthfully. It also benefits society: it reduces air and noise pollution, saving enormous amounts of money in healthcare costs. Protected bicycle lanes also reduce congestion, keep money in the local economy, reduces carbon emissions, and make neighborhoods more pleasant. The presence of protected bicycle lanes make streets safer, not only for cyclists, but also for motorists and pedestrians.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED

We use OpenStreetMap data to identify physically-protected bicycle facilities (those tagged as highway=cycleway or cycleway=track) and count the people living within a 300m walk of these facilities.

Policy Recommendations
  • Install protected bicycle lanes and/or add protection to existing lanes. Follow international quality standards.
  • Set up “quick build” or temporary cycle lanes, with the ability to transition them to permanent design.
  • Ensure bicycle lanes connect with greenways, low-speed low-volume streets, and cycle highways to form a network throughout the city.
  • Ensure lanes are well-lit, well-maintained, and reflect city conditions.
  • Redesign car-dominated streets as complete streets that prioritize safe cycling and walking.
  • Ensure that intersections are safe, including: Extending painted cycle lanes through intersections; Painting bicycle boxes in front of stopped traffic at intersections; Install bicycle traffic signals; Integrating fully protected intersection designs into plans for protected cycle lanes.
